PART 1 — PAPER SUMMARY
================================================================================

1.1 Key findings (headline)
---------------------------
A 12-LLM ensemble, aggregated by MEDIAN, achieves Brier 0.20 on 31 binary
Metaculus questions — statistically indistinguishable from the human crowd's
Brier 0.19. Significantly better than the 50% no-info baseline (Brier 0.25).

Previous best result was Schoenegger 2023 showing GPT-4 alone couldn't beat
the 50/50 baseline. This paper shows ensembling rescues the crowd-wisdom
property: aggregating heterogeneous LLM forecasts CANCELS individual biases
and produces a well-calibrated crowd-level estimate.

SECOND key finding: showing an LLM the MEDIAN HUMAN FORECAST improves its
Brier by 17-28%. But the LLM-after-update is still WORSE than a naive 50/50
linear pool of (LLM_before + human_median). So: do NOT let the LLM do its
own Bayesian update with external signals — apply linear pool externally.

1.2 Setup
---------
Dataset: 31 binary questions from a Metaculus 3-month tournament
   (Oct 2023 – Jan 2024). 925 human forecasters. 14/31 (~45%) resolved YES.
   Topics: geopolitics, finance, politics, AQI, crypto, space, etc.

Models: 12 LLMs (intentionally heterogeneous):
   GPT-4, GPT-4 with Bing, Claude 2, GPT-3.5-Turbo-Instruct, Solar-0-70B,
   Llama-2-70B, PaLM 2 (Chat-Bison@002), Coral (Command), Mistral-7B-Instruct,
   Bard (PaLM 2), Falcon-180B, Qwen-7B-Chat.

Per question: 3 independent queries × 12 models = up to 36 predictions.

Aggregation: median (within model across 3 queries) → median (across 12 models).
No Platt scaling, no extremization, no weighting. Pure median.

1.3 Per-model individual Brier scores
-------------------------------------
   GPT-4 (no Bing)            = 0.15  ← BEST individual
   GPT-4 (with Bing)          = 0.16
   Bard (PaLM 2)              = 0.19  ← ties human crowd
   Human crowd                = 0.19
   Falcon-180B                = 0.21
   Claude 2                   = 0.21
   Solar-0-70B                = 0.22
   PaLM 2 (Chat-Bison)        = 0.23
   Mistral-7B                 = 0.24
   Qwen-7B                    = 0.24
   GPT-3.5-Turbo              = 0.25
   Llama-2-70B                = 0.25
   Coral (Command)            = 0.38  ← outlier, drags ensemble down
   ENSEMBLE MEDIAN            = 0.20

KEY OBSERVATION: GPT-4 alone (0.15) beats the 12-model median (0.20) AND
beats the humans (0.19). The ensemble is dragged down by weaker models.
The "wisdom of crowds" smooths variance but the ceiling is the best model.

Implication for production: if you KNOW your best model, don't dilute it
with weaker ones unless you have a CALIBRATION need (the ensemble's
calibration is better than individual models — see 1.7).

1.4 What does diversity matter?
-------------------------------
Diversity dimensions in the ensemble:
   - Company (US/UK/CN/UAE/KR/FR): OpenAI, Anthropic, Google, Cohere, Meta,
     Mistral, Upstage, TII, Alibaba.
   - Internet access vs not: 3 with, 9 without.
   - Open vs closed weights: 4 open, 8 closed.
   - Model size: 7B → ~1.6T.

ANOVA across models: F(12,354) = 2.64, p = 0.002 — significant but driven
ENTIRELY by Coral being a bad outlier. Tukey HSD: no significant pair-wise
difference between any non-Coral models.

INTERNET ACCESS did NOT help in this paper:
   GPT-4 alone (0.15) < GPT-4 with Bing (0.16)
   Coral (Command) with internet = 0.38 (worst)
   Bard (PaLM 2) with internet = 0.19 (mid-pack)

This is suspicious — likely because (a) LLMs queried within 48h of question
open (limited news), (b) Bing/Bard implementations may not be optimized for
forecasting, (c) underpowered N. Halawi 2024 (with proper retrieval) DID
get gains from retrieval. The paper explicitly says retrieval gains are
orthogonal to ensemble gains.

1.5 Prompts used
----------------
Study 1: SINGLE FIXED PROMPT for all 12 models. Tetlock-style:
   - Role: "you are a superforecaster"
   - Reason about: comparison classes, base rates, past events, multiple
     perspectives
   - CoT trigger: "Take a deep breath and work on this problem step-by-step"
   - Output: number 0-100 to 2 decimal places
   - Inputs include Metaculus Background + Resolution criteria + Question

NO PROMPT-STYLE COMPARISON in Study 1.

Study 2 (longer prompt embedding Tetlock's "10 commandments"):
   - Triage, decompose, balance inside/outside, error balancing, etc.
   - Explicit mention of Brier scoring + calibration decomposition
   - Probability RANGE output (e.g. "between 30.00% and 45.00%"), midpoint
     = point estimate.

The longer Study 2 prompt yielded GPT-4 Brier 0.17, Claude 2 Brier 0.22.
Compare to Study 1: GPT-4 0.15, Claude 2 0.21. The bigger prompt did NOT
beat the simpler one in this paper — though no statistical comparison
offered.

PRACTICAL TAKEAWAY: There is no evidence the Tetlock-10-commandments
mega-prompt beats a simpler superforecaster prompt with CoT. Use the
simpler one to save tokens.

1.6 Human comparison & Study 2 (the linear-pool result)
-------------------------------------------------------
Study 2 — Intervene by showing the LLM the median human forecast, then
ask for an updated forecast.

   Model         Pre Brier   Post Brier   Improvement
   GPT-4         0.17        0.14         17.6% (p=0.003)
   Claude 2      0.22        0.15         28.6% (p<0.001)

Also confidence intervals tightened significantly (LLMs become more decisive
when given crowd input — both more accurate AND more confident).

BUT — and this is the killer result for ensemble design:
   The LLM-after-update is SIGNIFICANTLY WORSE than a naive
   0.5 × LLM_before + 0.5 × human_median pool:

   GPT-4: averaged Brier 0.13 vs updated Brier 0.14 (p=0.011)
   Claude 2: averaged 0.14 vs updated 0.15 (p<0.001)

A LINEAR POOL BEATS THE LLM'S OWN BAYESIAN-STYLE UPDATE.

Direct implication: when you have an EXTERNAL strong signal (market price,
crowd forecast, expert opinion), do NOT pass it into the LLM and ask it to
update. Apply the linear pool OUTSIDE the LLM call.

1.7 Calibration without correction
----------------------------------
Calibration Index (binned weighted MSE between predicted vs observed
frequencies), Table 3:
   Falcon-180B       0.027  ← best
   Qwen-7B           0.055
   PaLM 2            0.068
   Bard, Llama-2     0.071
   GPT-4             0.075
   Mistral, Solar    0.080
   Claude 2          0.082
   GPT-4 (Bing)      0.088
   GPT-3.5           0.106
   Coral             0.212  ← worst
   ENSEMBLE MEDIAN   0.041  ← second-best of any system measured

The ensemble's CALIBRATION (0.041) is BETTER than 11 of 12 individual
models. Aggregating biased forecasters cancels their biases.

The ensemble is still slightly overconfident (predicts events as more
likely than they actually occur — acquiescence bias).

Acquiescence bias is striking: across all 1,007 raw forecasts: median 60%,
mean 57.35% (vs base rate ~45%). LLMs systematically over-predict YES.

1.8 Untested but recommended
----------------------------
   ❌ Mean vs median vs trimmed-mean vs geometric-mean aggregations
   ❌ Extremization of the aggregate (Tetlock-style)
   ❌ Platt scaling on the aggregate
   ❌ Inverse-variance weighting (high-agreement → trust more)
   ❌ Per-category routing or weighting
   ❌ Pruning weak models by historical Brier
   ❌ Ensemble size curve (1 / 2 / 5 / 12 models)
   ❌ Retrieval-augmented ensembles (Halawi-style + Silicon-Crowd-style)

All of these are obvious extensions explicitly invited by the paper.

1.9 Limitations
---------------
   1. Coral and Bard had technical / content-policy issues (109 missing
      forecasts). Coral censorship and Falcon technical failures.
   2. Web-interface access only (no API) — default temperatures vary
      across providers, no temperature control.
   3. N=31 — small. Equivalence bounds wide (Cohen's d=0.5).
   4. Single 3-month window from late 2023 — may not generalize.
   5. No per-category breakdown.
   6. GPT-4 alone beats the ensemble. The headline "12 ≈ humans" is partly
      because the ensemble dilutes the best model.


PART 2 — HOW IT WORKS
================================================================================

For one question:
   1. Construct a Tetlock-style superforecaster prompt with the Metaculus
      Background + Resolution criteria + Question text.
   2. Query each of 12 LLMs three times with default temperature.
   3. For each LLM, take the median of its 3 forecasts (per-LLM denoising).
   4. Take the median of the 12 per-LLM medians (cross-LLM aggregation).
   5. Output as the ensemble prediction. No post-processing.

For Study 2's intervention:
   1. Get LLM's initial forecast.
   2. Reveal the median human forecast from Metaculus.
   3. Ask the LLM to re-forecast.
   4. (Better than #3: just compute 0.5 × initial + 0.5 × human externally.)


PART 3 — TRANSLATION TO PROPHET ARENA FORECAST TRACK
================================================================================

3.1 Concept-level translations
------------------------------
CONCEPT 1: Median > Mean for aggregation (citation-supported).
   Our v8 currently does cross-model MEAN inside the meta-reasoner (the
   meta sees probs + reasoning and outputs a consensus). The Silicon Crowd
   paper says simple MEDIAN of independent forecasts BEATS the LLM's own
   aggregation effort.
   → Add a MEDIAN as a fallback / sanity check in v8. If meta-reasoner's
     consensus is FAR from the per-agent median, distrust it.

CONCEPT 2: LLMs can match human-crowd Brier with ZERO retrieval.
   The 12-LLM ensemble at 0.20 Brier on Metaculus, no retrieval. That's
   an upper bound on what no-retrieval can do. We're at 0.39 on smoke
   (with retrieval). Suggests our retrieval pipeline is currently HURTING
   on some events (e.g., niche primaries where Tavily returns irrelevant
   stuff). Worth ablating: try a no-retrieval v8 variant.

CONCEPT 3: Linear pool > LLM-internal Bayesian update.
   When mixing market price + LLM forecast, do NOT pass the market into the
   LLM and ask it to incorporate. Apply the linear pool externally.
   Our v8 ALREADY DOES THIS CORRECTLY:
      blended = alpha * kalshi_price + (1-alpha) * llm_prob
   Don't change this. (This was the right call in retrospect.)

CONCEPT 4: GPT-4-class models alone outperform diluted ensembles.
   Our v8 ensemble (5 slots) includes GPT-5 and Gemini alongside Opus.
   On Silicon Crowd data, GPT-4 alone was best; ensemble was 2nd-best.
   For Prophet Arena, the question is whether DIVERSITY of failure modes
   buys more than DILUTION of best model.
   → Empirically test: run v8 with ONLY Opus 4.7 × 3 prompts (CoT, ToT,
     Calibrated) — 3 slots, same model, different prompts. Compare to v8
     with 5 slots × different models. See which Brier wins on smoke.

CONCEPT 5: Acquiescence bias is systematic.
   LLMs mean-predict 57% YES vs actual 45%. We should:
      → Track our v8's mean prediction on live data
      → If consistently above the base rate, apply a corrective shift

3.2 Technique-level changes to v8
---------------------------------
Change 1: Add median as fallback consensus.
   When meta-reasoner's output disagrees with the per-agent median by >0.15,
   fall back to the median. This is similar to AIA's
   high-confidence-vs-fall-back-to-mean pattern but uses cross-agent median.

   Implementation: in agent_v8.predict(), after meta_reason() returns,
   compute median over agent_results. If
      |meta_probs[top_outcome] - median_probs[top_outcome]| > 0.15
   then use median_probs instead.

Change 2: Test a no-retrieval ablation.
   Set FORECAST_MAX_RESULTS_PER_QUERY=0 (or skip Tavily entirely) and re-run
   smoke. If Brier improves or stays roughly equal, the retrieval is net
   neutral / harmful on this distribution. Then we can save the Tavily
   integration cost.

Change 3: Same-model-different-prompts variant.
   Build v8-alt: 5 slots all using anthropic/claude-opus-4.7 with 5
   different prompts (CoT, ToT, Calibrated, Devil's-advocate, Reference-class).
   Compare Brier to current v8 (5 different models, mostly same prompt).
   The "different model" diversity is meant to cancel different biases.
   The "different prompt" diversity targets different reasoning paths.

Change 4: Acquiescence-correction shift.
   After Platt scaling, if mean prediction across outcomes for binary
   questions is consistently >0.50 (vs base rate <0.50), apply a small
   uniform downward shift on probability-of-yes outcomes.

3.3 Prophet API handling
------------------------
   • No structural changes to /predict endpoint or response shape.
   • The Silicon Crowd's "human median" doesn't exist on Prophet Arena
     (we have Kalshi prices, which are the equivalent). Already handled
     via linear pool.
   • Sample-size warning: the paper's 31 questions is too small to draw
     strong conclusions. Our 26-event smoke is similar. Both are
     UNDERPOWERED for picking between aggregators. Live eval has ~200
     events over 2 weeks — better signal.

3.4 Specific recipes worth copying
----------------------------------
   • Tetlock-style superforecaster prompt with CoT trigger. We already
     have this in v8's Calibrated/CoT/ToT prompts. The Silicon Crowd's
     more elaborate "10-commandments" prompt did NOT beat the simpler
     version. Keep our current prompts (don't over-engineer).
   • Multi-query single-model temperature sampling. The paper queries each
     LLM 3 times and takes the median to denoise. We could do this on Opus
     (the strongest) to reduce within-model variance.
   • Track mean prediction across all questions as an acquiescence signal.


PART 4 — PROS AND CONS for adopting Silicon Crowd patterns
================================================================================

PROS
----
1. SIMPLEST RECIPE in the literature: just take median across heterogeneous
   models. Robust, theoretically grounded (wisdom of crowds), works without
   retrieval.
2. CALIBRATION IS A FREE LUNCH from ensembling — aggregate CI of 0.041
   beats every individual model. Less need for aggressive Platt.
3. LINEAR-POOL RESULT validates our v8 approach to Kalshi blending.
4. WARNING about asking LLMs to do their own aggregation — supports our
   choice NOT to rely solely on meta-reasoner.
5. MEDIAN OPERATION can be added in 5 lines to v8 as a sanity-check
   fallback.

CONS
----
1. SMALL N (31 questions) — equivalence bounds are wide. Result may not
   generalize to Prophet Arena's distribution.
2. NO RETRIEVAL — paper's main thesis is "LLMs alone match humans" which
   may not be true on harder/fresher questions Prophet Arena throws at us.
   Halawi-style retrieval-augmented systems should outperform.
3. NO ENSEMBLE SIZE CURVE — we don't know if 12 > 5 > 2.
4. INTERNET ACCESS NOT VALIDATED. The paper's 3-model-internet vs
   9-no-internet experiment is null but underpowered.
5. THE ENSEMBLE LOSES TO GPT-4 ALONE on this data. Could be the wrong
   architecture if we have a clearly-best base model.
6. NO CATEGORY BREAKDOWN — can't tell if median fails on specific question
   types.
7. PROMPT-STYLE UNTESTED — Tetlock-10-commandments vs simpler prompt not
   compared rigorously. We use Tetlock-style; might be wasted tokens.
8. ALL FORECAST DATA IS OLD (late 2023). Models tested are mostly
   superseded (GPT-4, Claude 2). Modern models (Opus 4.7, GPT-5) may have
   different ensemble dynamics.

  ☐ Run a same-model-different-prompts variant (Opus × 5 prompts). Compare
    to current v8.

Priority 3 (LOWER PRIORITY):
  ☐ Add 3-query temperature denoising on Opus (sample 3× at temp=0.7, take

  ☐ Add geometric-mean and trimmed-mean as alternative aggregators in v8;
    log all three, see which generalizes best on live data.
